# Product Positioning — Reasoning Structure

## Step 1: Competitive Landscape
- List what customers currently use to solve this problem
- Include direct competitors, indirect alternatives, and "do nothing"
- For each: identify the key weakness or gap
- Understand why customers tolerate current solutions

## Step 2: Unique Attributes
- List capabilities that ONLY this product has
- Filter: remove features that competitors also have
- For each unique attribute: why is it hard to copy?
- These become your defensible differentiators

## Step 3: Value Mapping
- Map each unique attribute to a customer need or pain point
- Group into 2-3 value themes (e.g., "speed", "simplicity", "intelligence")
- Each theme should resonate with the target customer's language
- Prioritize themes by customer impact, not product complexity

## Step 4: Target Customer Definition
- Define the ideal customer profile precisely
- Include: role, company size, budget, pain severity, buying trigger
- Define disqualifying criteria (who is NOT a good fit)
- The narrower the target, the sharper the positioning

## Step 5: Category Choice
- Choose the market category carefully
- Options: existing category, sub-category, or new category
- Existing category: leverage familiarity, but compete on known dimensions
- New category: own the space, but bear education cost
- Choose based on your unique attributes and customer awareness

## Step 6: Positioning Statement
- Template: For [target customer], [product] is the [category] that [key benefit] unlike [alternatives] because [unique attribute]
- Must be one sentence
- Must be falsifiable (a competitor could NOT say the same thing)
- Must be meaningful to the target customer
